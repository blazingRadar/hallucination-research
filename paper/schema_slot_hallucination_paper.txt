Title

When the Schema is the Prompt: A Small Result on Field-Label-Induced Hallucination

Subtitle

In a 12-anchor paired ablation across three frontier models, named output-schema
slots produced fabrications even when the prompt explicitly excluded identifiers
and citations. Neutral descriptive slots stayed at zero in both exclusion
conditions. A larger V6 stress test then found an ordered dose signal
concentrated in OpenAI outputs, while narrowly missing the preregistered pooled
effect-size threshold.

Draft

Schema field names are prompt text.

That is the shortest version of this result. In a small paired ablation across
`gpt-5-chat-latest`, `claude-sonnet-4-6`, and `grok-4.3`, output schemas that
asked for named artifacts produced hallucination-positive responses in 10/36
responses without explicit anti-identifier exclusions and 14/36 responses with
those exclusions. Neutral descriptive schemas produced 0/36 in both conditions.

The extra clause did not suppress the effect. I do not read the movement from
10/36 to 14/36 as evidence that exclusions made the problem worse; the Wilson
intervals overlap heavily. The calibrated reading is narrower and stronger
enough: on this corpus, adding natural-language exclusions did not fix the
named-slot failure mode.

The fields that caused trouble looked like normal structured-output fields:

- `study_name`
- `rule_identifier`
- `benchmark_name`
- `advisory_id`
- `framework_name`
- `document_name`

The neutral fields looked like:

- `pattern`
- `mechanism`
- `evidence_strength`
- `limitation`
- `uncertainty`

The prompt body did not contain the fabricated entity. There was no `type:
explain` operator. The domain was real. The output schema was the part applying
pressure.

This is not a general solution to hallucination. It is not a production
benchmark. It is not enough for a paper. It is a narrow result about one
failure mode in structured prompts and tool-like output schemas.

But I think it matters because structured outputs are becoming the default way
models connect to software.

V6 extends the result but also narrows it. On a fresh 24-anchor corpus with 0,
1, 2, 4, or 8 named-artifact schema slots, the pooled dual-judge positive rates
were ordered: 0/144, 0/144, 0/144, 4/144, and 20/144. The trend survived
prompt-length adjustment, but the preregistered 8-slot minus 0-slot effect-size
bar was narrowly missed: observed 0.139 against a required 0.15. The effect was
provider-conditional: OpenAI carried the trend, while Claude produced no
positives and xAI produced one dose-4 positive and no dose-8 positives.

## The result

The current closed 2x2 is:

| Schema slot type | Explicit anti-identifier exclusion? | Hallucination-positive |
|---|---:|---:|
| Named entity slots | No | 10/36 |
| Named entity slots | Yes | 14/36 |
| Neutral descriptive slots | Yes | 0/36 |
| Neutral descriptive slots | No | 0/36 |

The Wilson 95% intervals are:

- Named slots without exclusions: 10/36, Wilson 95% [0.158, 0.440]
- Named slots with exclusions: 14/36, Wilson 95% [0.248, 0.551]
- Neutral slots with exclusions: 0/36, Wilson 95% [0.000, 0.096]
- Neutral slots without exclusions: 0/36, Wilson 95% [0.000, 0.096]

All runs used the same three providers, temperature 0, the same 12 anchor
domains, and prompts where the false anchor and `type:` operator were absent.

The V5C follow-up was the important counterargument test. Before V5C, the
obvious skeptical reply was: maybe neutral slots only looked safe because they
included an explicit instruction not to output identifiers or citations. V5B
addressed that on the neutral side. V5C addressed it on the named side.

The result was not "exclusions solve the problem." Named slots still produced
14/36 AND-agreement positives with explicit exclusions.

V5C also made the old prompt-length objection less threatening. The two
closest prompt-length cells were V5C named-with-exclude at about 272 characters
on average and V5 neutral-with-exclude at about 279 characters. Those two cells
produced 14/36 and 0/36 respectively. That is not a fully length-matched
experiment, but it makes prompt length unlikely to be the load-bearing variable
on this corpus.

V6 tested a larger dose-response version of the same concern. The fresh V6
corpus used five named-slot doses:

| Named slots | Positive | N | Rate | Wilson 95% CI |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 0 | 0 | 144 | 0.000 | [0.000, 0.026] |
| 1 | 0 | 144 | 0.000 | [0.000, 0.026] |
| 2 | 0 | 144 | 0.000 | [0.000, 0.026] |
| 4 | 4 | 144 | 0.028 | [0.011, 0.069] |
| 8 | 20 | 144 | 0.139 | [0.092, 0.205] |

The named-slot coefficient stayed positive in the length-covariate logistic
model (p=1.9e-06), while prompt length itself was not a significant predictor
(p=0.326). The important boundary is that V6 did not meet its preregistered
binary success threshold: the planned 8-slot minus 0-slot difference was >=
0.15, and the observed difference was 0.139.

## What counted as a positive

The primary label was dual-judge AND-agreement hallucination-positive. OpenAI
and Anthropic judges both had to mark the row positive.

Positive labels were:

- `accepted_false_premise`
- `fabricated_specifics`
- `fabricated_citation`

Misrecalling a real entity was not counted as hallucination-positive. Ambiguous
cases were not positive under the primary rule.

Examples from the V5C positive log show the shape of the failure. A prompt with
`study_name` elicited fake study-like rows. A prompt with `benchmark_name`
elicited benchmark-like names. A prompt with `advisory_id` elicited advisory-like
identifiers. In several cases the model tried to satisfy the exclusion by using
generic placeholders or sanitized labels, but still filled the named artifact
slot with a concrete-looking entity.

That is the thing I want to point at: the schema did not merely format the
answer. It helped define what kind of object the model thought it was supposed
to produce.

## Distribution

The V5C positives were not all from one model:

- OpenAI: 6/12
- Anthropic: 5/12
- xAI: 3/12

They were not all from one anchor either. The 14 positives appeared across 7 of
the 12 anchors:

- VG005: 3
- VG011: 3
- VG008: 2
- VG009: 2
- VG010: 2
- VG001: 1
- VG012: 1

That does not prove broad generality. It does make the result less likely to be
a single-provider or single-anchor accident.

Judge disagreement was non-trivial in V5C: 11/36 rows had either primary-label
or positive-label disagreement. The AND-agreement rule is conservative for the
headline count, but the disagreement rate is still important. The later audits
kept the conservative AND-agreement framing rather than treating single-judge
positives as the headline result.

V6 changed the distribution story. The dose signal was not cross-provider in
the strong sense. It was concentrated in `gpt-5-chat-latest`: OpenAI produced
0/48, 0/48, 0/48, 3/48, and 20/48 positives across doses 0, 1, 2, 4, and 8.
Claude produced zero positives at every dose. xAI produced one dose-4 positive
and zero dose-8 positives. That makes V6 an OpenAI-specific schema-slot
pressure result, not a clean three-provider replication.

## How I got here

This started from a simpler observation. If a prompt asserts a false entity and
asks a model to explain it, models often complete the pattern instead of
checking whether the entity exists.

That first observation was not publishable as a novel result. Verification-first
prompting already has a literature, and my first formal experiment was too
close to a small Chain-of-Verification replication. I closed that version rather
than ship a result that did not deserve the framing.

Then the open-structure control failed.

I removed the fabricated entity from the prompt. I removed the forced `type:
explain` operator. I expected hallucination to fall to the floor. It did not.
The prompts were "clean" in the obvious sense, but the output schema still had
fields like `study_name`, `rule_identifier`, `benchmark_name`, and
`advisory_id`.

The models filled those slots.

That failure was more useful than the original expected success. It suggested a
third pressure source: not just false premise in the prompt body, not just task
operator, but the lexical semantics of the output schema itself.

The later experiments narrowed that:

- V4 exposed the schema leak.
- V4B replaced named slots with descriptive slots and got 0/36, but it changed
  too many things at once.
- V5 ran named slots and neutral slots in a paired single run: 10/36 vs 0/36.
- V5B removed explicit anti-identifier exclusions from the neutral arm: still
  0/36.
- V5C ran the missing named-with-exclude cell: 14/36.
- V6 ran a fresh-corpus dose-response stress test: pooled rates increased
  0/144, 0/144, 0/144, 4/144, 20/144 across 0, 1, 2, 4, and 8 named slots, but
  the preregistered pooled effect-size threshold was narrowly missed and the
  result was concentrated in OpenAI outputs.

That is the current evidence chain.

## What I think is happening

My current hypothesis is schema-slot pressure.

A field named `study_name` does not merely say "format the answer in this
column." It says "there is a study-shaped thing to retrieve or produce." If the
domain is real but recall is weak, the model may satisfy the shape with a
plausible object.

A field named `pattern` or `mechanism` asks for a different kind of answer. It
does not require a named artifact. The model can answer from general knowledge
without inventing a specific study, advisory, benchmark, statute, or rule ID.

This distinction matters for structured-output APIs. We often treat field names
as inert developer labels. They are not inert. They are part of the prompt.

## What this does not show

This result has real limits.

First, this is one 12-anchor synthetic corpus. It should not be treated as a
general benchmark.

Second, prompt length is not fully controlled. V5C weakens the length-confound
story because the named-with-exclude and neutral-with-exclude cells were close
in character length while splitting 14/36 vs 0/36, but a future version should
still use deliberately length-matched variants.

Third, V6 has now tested a dose-response curve on a fresh 24-anchor corpus. The
pooled rate increased monotonically, and the trend survived length-as-covariate
adjustment. The claim remains narrow because the preregistered pooled
effect-size threshold was missed and the result was provider-conditional.

Fourth, this is not production tool-schema evidence. The prompts are structured
queries, not live function-calling schemas inside production agent loops.

Fifth, neutral schemas do not eliminate all possible errors. One V5B
disagreement surfaced unsupported quantitative claims in a neutral response.
The present claim is about named artifact fabrication under this scoring rule,
not about all factual error.

Sixth, V5C was a follow-up run, not part of the original V5 batch. It used the
same models, providers, corpus family, temperature setting, and scoring rule,
but it is still a follow-up cell.

Seventh, V6 does not isolate slot count from field-vocabulary semantics. Higher
named-slot doses introduced additional labels such as `citation_label`,
`identifier_code`, `program_name`, `standard_name`, `dataset_name`, and
`document_name`. That means V6 tests increasing named-artifact schema pressure,
not pure slot count in isolation.

Eighth, V6 should not be pooled with V5/V5B/V5C as if they used the same
vocabulary. V6 used a generic dose vocabulary, while the V5 family used
per-anchor named slots.

## Related work and positioning

Verification-first prompting and Chain-of-Verification style methods are the
closest prior family, which is why I closed the V3 verification-gate result
rather than publish it as novel. TruthfulQA and hallucination benchmarks measure
model truthfulness under question framing, but they do not isolate output field
label semantics in this way.

The V6 framing is also consistent with two newer pieces of context. Kalai et
al. 2025, "Why Language Models Hallucinate," characterizes OpenAI-class
training as rewarding guessing under some evaluation regimes. An
Anthropic-OpenAI joint pilot evaluation reported high Claude refusal behavior on
hallucination evaluations. I cite these as positioning, not as proof of my
result: V6 is a controlled measurement of a schema-slot pressure condition where
that provider asymmetry appears sharply.

Phare V2 is also relevant because it studies structured tool-call failure
modes. My V6 setup is complementary: it tests parameter or field addition
pressure rather than omission alone.

## Why I am still publishing this

I am publishing this because the failure mode is concrete and because the audit
trail made the claim narrower rather than larger.

The lab repeatedly caught itself overclaiming:

- V3 was closed as a CoVe-like replication.
- V4 failed and exposed schema leakage.
- V4B overclaimed a joint-removal result.
- V5 paired the ablation.
- V5B closed the neutral exclusion-clause confound.
- V5C closed the named-with-exclude cell.
- V6 added a larger dose-response stress test and forced the claim to become
  provider-conditional.

The result is now smaller than the original story, but more useful.

I do not think the takeaway is "use my magic prompt." I think the takeaway is:
if your schema asks for named artifacts in domains where the model may not know
the answer, you may be creating a fabrication request.

The practical implication is simple. Schema and prompt linters should flag
fields like:

- `study_name`
- `citation`
- `rule_id`
- `benchmark_name`
- `advisory_id`
- `statute_name`

They should suggest descriptive alternatives unless retrieval or validation is
available downstream.

## Next experiment

V6 is complete. The next version should be boring and cleaner, but only if
future technical work justifies extending this bounded artifact:

- fresh corpus
- length-matched prompts
- larger high-dose sample
- dose 12 and dose 16
- isolation of field vocabulary from slot count
- production-style tool/function schemas
- human adjudication of judge disagreements

Until then, the current result is bounded evidence for one specific point:

Schema field names are prompt text. Treat them like prompt text.
